  • Basics

  • What is the QSR1¬Æ and what is it used for?

    The QSR1¬Æ is an ultrasonic guided wave scanner that can measure the minimum remaining and nominal pipe wall thicknesses. The circumferential scanner uses circumferential guided waves and advanced frequency analysis to quantitatively and accurately measure the minimum remaining and nominal pipe wall thicknesses. These measurement data are highly repeatable and reliable because of the QSR1¬Æ's Auto-Calibration and Semi-Automatic Scanning capabilities. The circumferential scanner is mainly used for the sizing of corrosion under pipe supports (CUPS) or touch point corrosion.

  • What does QSR stand for?

    QSR is an abbreviation of Quantitative Short Range. It is currently a classification of product under that uses GUL Scanning technology to quantitatively size corrosion within a relatively short inspection range.

  • What is the range of pipe sizes and thicknesses that can be inspected using the QSR1¬Æ?

    The QSR1¬Æ can accomodate for any pipe sizes from NPS 6" to 24" (DN 150 to 600) with nominal pipe wall thicknesses ranging from 6 mm to 13 mm (1/4" to 1/2"); custom sizes for any pipe size between NPS 6" to 24" can also be made.

  • Which type(s) of pipe support is the QSR1¬Æ applicable for?

    The QSR1¬Æ circumferential scanner is applicable for simple resting pipe supports and any pipe supports which does not hinder the movement of the circumferential scanner over the pipe section with the support.

  • Technology

  • How does QSR1¬Æ Circumferential Scanning work?

    The QSR1¬Æ circumferential scanner transmits shear guided waves, which is low frequency (hundreds of kHz) ultrasound, that travels around the pipe circumference. The reflection and transmission of the waves are measured by the receiver sensor and analysed using GUL Scanning algorithm that is based on a frequency-based method.

  • What is Frequency Analysis and why is it better than Amplitude analysis?

    Conventional NDT techniques, such as MRUT, typically use Amplitude-based methods, which involves estimating defect sizes based on the amplitude of the signals. However, there are many external factors that can affect the amplitude of the guided waves and hence affecting the accuracy of the sizing estimate. The 3 most common factors are the corrosion geometry (length and shape), leaking of energy into the pipe's liquid load and attenuation into support. For more information, watch this video.



    The QSR1¬Æ uses a frequency-based method which directly relates the frequency properties of the waves to the pipe wall thickness. This means that it is significantly less affected by amplitude reductions caused by attenuation or corrosion shape. In addition, the QSR1¬Æ uses the Shear Horizontal guided waves which do not leak energy into liquid loadings in pipes. Hence, the QSR1¬Æ's corrosion sizing capability provides a truly quantitative and is more accurate than other amplitude-based methods.

  • What is the wall or corrosion sizing accuracy and resolution of the QSR1¬Æ?

    The accuracy is typically ±0.5 mm (< 50% wall thickness) and ±1 mm (> 50% wall thickness), based on rigorous lab tests conducted on an NPS 10 Schedule 40 pipe. The measurement resolution is ± 0.01 mm.

  • What is the minimum scanning resolution?

    The minimum scanning resolution is± 0.5 cm.

  • Application

  • Can the QSR1¬Æ move or scan over welds?

    Circumferential Girth welds - Generally no but it depends on the height of the weld cap; yes, if the welds have been grinded flush with the pipe and the QSR1¬Æ can move across it without restrictions.

    Long Seam welds or spiral welds - Either types of seams do not matter for results; but the sensor carts have problem driving over the hump of the weld cap.

  • Metals - what metals etc can we inspect? Can we do composite or other materials. Aluminium?

    The QSR1¬Æ is designed to work on carbon steel pipes.
