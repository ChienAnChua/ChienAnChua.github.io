  • Basics

  • What is the QSR1¬Æ and what is it used for?

    The QSR1¬Æ is a guided wave scanner that can measure the minimum remaining and nominal pipe wall thicknesses. The circumferential scanner uses circumferential guided waves and advanced frequency analysis to quantitatively and accurately measure wall thicknesses. These measurement data are highly repeatable and reliable because of the Auto-Calibration and Semi-Automatic Scanning capabilities. The circumferential scanner is mainly used for the sizing of corrosion under pipe supports (CUPS) or touch point corrosion.

  • What does QSR stand for?

    QSR is an abbreviation of Quantitative Short Range. It is a group of products that uses patented GUL Scanning technology to quantitatively size corrosion within a relatively short inspection range.

  • What results can the QSR1¬Æ give me?

    The most important results that the QSR1¬Æ gives is the plot of minimum remaining wall thickness against pipe axial distance. These results can be used to perform fitness-for-service (FFS) assessment (e.g. ASME B31G) on the corroded section of a pipe. In addition, the QSR1¬Æ also provides measurement of the nominal wall thickness along the scanned length.



  • What is the range of pipe sizes and thicknesses that can be inspected using the QSR1¬Æ?

    The QSR1¬Æ can accommodate for any pipe sizes from NPS 6" to 24" (DN 150 to 600), with nominal pipe wall thicknesses ranging from 6 mm to 13 mm (1/4" to 1/2"). Any custom pipe sizes between NPS 6" to 24" can also be made to order. The diameter of the pipe to be inspected shall determine the choice of frame size to use. For example, to inspect an NPS 10 pipe, the 10 inch frames should be used.



  • What type of pipe support is the QSR1¬Æ applicable for?

    The QSR1¬Æ circumferential scanner is applicable for simple resting pipe supports and any pipe supports which does not hinder the movement of the circumferential scanner over the pipe section with the support.

  • Technology

  • How does QSR1¬Æ Circumferential Scanning work?

    The QSR1¬Æ circumferential scanner transmits shear guided waves, which is low frequency (hundreds of kHz) ultrasound, that travels around the pipe circumference. The reflection and transmission of the waves are measured by the receiver sensor and analysed using GUL Scanning algorithm that is based on a frequency-based method.



    The QSR1¬Æ is designed to semi-automatically move along the pipe and collect wall thickness measurements at fixed scanning intervals along the pipe. See our demo video for more information.



  • What is Frequency Analysis and why is it better than Amplitude analysis?

    The QSR1¬Æ uses a frequency-based method which directly relates the frequency properties of the waves to the pipe wall thickness. In contrast to this, conventional NDT techniques, such as MRUT, typically use Amplitude-based methods, which involves estimating defect sizes based on the amplitude of the signals.



    However, there are many external factors that can affect the amplitude of the guided waves and hence affecting the accuracy of the sizing estimate. The first important variable is the corrosion profile. If analysing amplitde alone, a deep but narrow profile could give the same amplitude reduction as a corrosion with a shallow but wide profile. This is because the guided wave reflection ratio is a function of both length and depth of the corrosion. However, frequency analysis can be used to directly measure the minimum remaining wall thickness because it possesses a unique frequency signature.



    The second factor to consider is the effects of signal energy loss on the corrosion sizing accuracy. Energy losses due to paint, coatings, corrosion scale and support pads can cause a reduction in reflection amplitude. This amplitude drop can lead to a false positive result or large inaccuracies when sizing a defect using amplitude analysis. Frequency analysis methods are significantly less affected by the signal energy losses as the frequency content or signature is not affected; only the amplitudes.



    In addition, the QSR1¬Æ uses the Shear Horizontal guided waves which do not leak energy into liquid loadings in pipes. Hence, the QSR1¬Æ's corrosion sizing capability provides a truly quantitative and is more accurate than other amplitude-based methods.

  • What is the wall or corrosion sizing accuracy and resolution of the QSR1¬Æ?

    The accuracy is typically ± 0.5 mm (< 50% wall thickness) and ±1 mm (> 50% wall thickness), based on rigorous lab tests conducted on an NPS 10 Schedule 40 pipe. The measurement resolution is ± 0.01 mm.



  • What is the minimum scanning resolution?

    The highest scanning resolution is ± 0.5 cm.

  • Application

  • Can the QSR1¬Æ move or scan over welds?

    Circumferential Girth welds - Generally no but it depends on the height of the weld cap; yes, if the welds have been grinded flush with the pipe and the QSR1¬Æ can move across it without restrictions.
    Long Seam welds or spiral welds - Either types of seams do not matter for results; but the sensor carts have problems driving over the hump of the weld cap.

    The QSR1¬Æ is designed to work on carbon steel pipes.
